Theodore Roosevelt is credited with the observation that *comparison is the thief of joy* — and nowhere does that theft happen more quietly, or more completely, than in our relationship with beauty.

We compare flowers to pearls, youth to age, the pink lady apple's blushing skin to silver and leather gloves. Each thing lovely in itself becomes a contestant in a competition it never entered.

There is the beauty of the natural world — the way lilacs smell after rain, the geometry of a spider's web at dawn, the color of Colorado sky in October. There is the beauty of made objects — good leather, hand-thrown pottery, a well-worn wooden spoon. There is the beauty of human beings at every age, which comparison flattens into a single, narrow window.

The practice of art teaches something about this. When you look long enough at anything — a still life, a face, a patch of sky — comparison falls away and you are left with the thing itself, glowing with its own particular light. That is the moment the painting begins.

To see without comparison is an act of attention, and attention is the beginning of love.

*Look at the pink lady apple. Not in relation to anything else. Just: the pink lady apple.*
