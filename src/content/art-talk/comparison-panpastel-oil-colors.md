Many artists work in multiple media, and one of the recurring frustrations is translating color choices from one medium to another. After years of working with both PanPastel and M. Graham oil paints, I have assembled a color comparison that I hope will be useful.

## Why This Matters

Color charts tell you what a color actually looks like — not what the name promises. The same name (say, "Burnt Sienna") can look very different across manufacturers and media. M. Graham oils are walnut-oil based, which gives them a characteristic saturation and transparency. PanPastel has its own unique behavior: semi-transparent, mixable, and buildable in layers.

## Key Correspondences

| PanPastel | M. Graham Equivalent | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Yellow Ochre | Yellow Ochre | Very close match |
| Burnt Sienna | Burnt Sienna | M. Graham is slightly more orange |
| Raw Umber | Raw Umber | Excellent match |
| Phthalo Blue | Phthalo Blue (GS) | Nearly identical |
| Ultramarine Blue | Ultramarine Blue | Close; M. Graham slightly deeper |
| Quinacridone Magenta | Quinacridone Magenta | Excellent |
| Pyrrole Red | Napthol Red | Close but not identical |
| Titanium White | Titanium White | Good match |
| Carbon Black | Ivory Black | M. Graham is warmer |

## How to Use This Chart

Before beginning a painting, I make small sample squares of both media side by side. This takes ten minutes and saves hours of adjustment later.

The goal is not perfect matching — the media will always behave differently. The goal is to understand the relationship so that your color choices are intentional rather than accidental.
