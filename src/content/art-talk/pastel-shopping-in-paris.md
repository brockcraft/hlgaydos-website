It really is too much to take in all at once. To think that I am standing on the same oak floors in front of the same wooden drawers, each holding a different color pastel, that artists have been opening for over a hundred years.

The store is narrow, dark, and smells of chalk and dust and wood — the smell of all the art that has been made from what is sold here. On the street outside, Paris moves at its ordinary pace. In here, time is different.

I was thinking about style — or rather, about courage. It takes courage to develop a style, to commit to an aesthetic vision so completely that it becomes recognizable. The woman I had seen in the street, perfectly dressed, not young but ageless — she had it. The style was not about the clothes exactly; it was about the decision to be, completely, herself.

I thought of Theodore Roosevelt's observation: *comparison is the thief of joy*. She was not comparing herself to a younger version, or to fashion, or to anyone else. She simply was.

Back at the drawer of terre verte, I stopped comparing and started choosing. The pale greens and warm grays, the colors that belonged to this light, this city, this moment.

That, I thought, is the lesson.
