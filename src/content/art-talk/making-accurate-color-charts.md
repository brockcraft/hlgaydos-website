A color chart is one of the most useful tools a pastel painter can have. It shows you exactly what a color looks like on your chosen surface, how it mixes with its neighbors, and what happens when you add white or black.

## What Is PanPastel?

PanPastel is a unique pastel medium: it's a highly pigmented pastel in a compact pan form, applied with sponge-tipped tools (Sofft Tools). Unlike stick pastels, it blends and applies more like paint. It is semi-transparent, which means layering is essential.

## Materials You Will Need

- PanPastel colors (see below for a starter set)
- Sofft Tools (sponge applicators — get both the round and flat styles)
- Pastel-compatible paper (Pastelmat, Canson Mi-Teintes, or similar)
- Mr. Clean Magic Eraser for cleaning tools between colors
- A ruled template for your chart squares

## The Master Palette System

Rather than making a chart of every color you own, start with a **master palette** — a curated set of 12–16 colors that can mix to create almost any color you need. Include:

- One warm and one cool version of each primary (yellow, red, blue)
- An earth yellow (Yellow Ochre or Raw Sienna)
- An earth red (Burnt Sienna)
- A neutral dark (Payne's Grey or Raw Umber)
- Titanium White
- Carbon Black (use sparingly)

## Step-by-Step Process

1. **Draw your template.** Rule a grid of 1-inch squares on pastel paper. Label each row with a color name.
2. **Apply full-strength color.** Using a clean Sofft Tool, apply each color at full strength in the first square.
3. **Mix with neighbors.** In subsequent squares, mix that color with adjacent colors on the wheel.
4. **Add white.** Show what each color looks like tinted with white.
5. **Add black.** Show the shade (use sparingly — PanPastel's Payne's Grey is often better than black).
6. **Note your observations.** Which colors granulate? Which are transparent? Which are opaque?

Clean your tools between every color application. The Mr. Clean Magic Eraser is remarkably effective for this.

## Half-Tones and Value

Understanding value — the lightness or darkness of a color — is more important than hue. When making your charts, squint at them. If all your colors collapse to a similar gray, you have good value range. If everything looks the same value, that is the problem to solve.

The color chart will show you this immediately.
