Pastel pencils are an indispensable complement to soft pastels and PanPastel. They allow for fine detail, controlled line work, and precise marks that are impossible with a soft stick or sponge. But they vary enormously in quality, hardness, and sharpenability.

Here is a comparison of the three brands I have worked with most closely.

## Carbothello Pastel Pencils (Stabilo)

**Character:** Medium-soft, good pigmentation, reliable.

Carbothello pencils are the workhorses of the pastel pencil world. They sharpen reasonably well, hold a point better than softer options, and come in a good range of colors. The pigmentation is solid. They are easy to find and reasonably priced.

**Best for:** General detail work, layering over soft pastels, everyday use.

## Caran d'Ache Pastel Pencils

**Character:** Very soft, beautiful color, difficult to sharpen.

Swiss-made Caran d'Ache pencils have an exceptionally smooth, buttery feel and extraordinary color saturation. The downside: they are the most difficult to sharpen without breaking, and the core can crumble. Use them for broad marks rather than fine lines.

**Best for:** Blending, soft atmospheric effects, final color touches.

## Conté Pastel Pencils

**Character:** Harder, more precise, excellent color range.

Conté pencils are firmer than the others and hold a sharper point. This makes them excellent for fine detail work and initial sketching. The trade-off is that the harder consistency can scratch the surface if applied too aggressively in early layers.

**Best for:** Underpainting, fine detail, architectural elements, drawing into a composition.

## Sharpener Recommendations

- An electric sharpener is safest for the softest pencils (Caran d'Ache especially).
- A hand-held cosmetic sharpener with a long barrel works well for Carbothello.
- Always sharpen slowly and with light pressure — the cores are fragile.
- Sandpaper blocks (nail files) work beautifully for touching up a point without risk.
